The Undeleted Problem: A Case Study in Anti-AI Radicalization Among Youth Developers

🔍 Anonymous Researcher 📅 June 22, 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read

Executive Summary

Over the past several months, an individual operating under the alias "Undeleted" has emerged as a case study in how young developers can become radicalized against artificial intelligence tools. This report examines her trajectory, vocabulary, behavioral patterns, and the broader implications for the developer community.

The subject is a 17-year-old developer who maintains multiple open-source projects on GitHub. She is active on Discord under the handle usr.undeleted. What began as ordinary teenage programming has evolved into something more concerning: a systematic rejection of AI-assisted development coupled with increasingly hostile terminology directed at the technology and its practitioners.

Anti-AI Vocabulary

The subject has developed a distinct lexicon for discussing AI and its output. This vocabulary appears to serve both as in-group signaling and as a mechanism for dehumanizing the technology — making it easier to justify rejection without technical evaluation.

"Clanker"

The subject's preferred term for AI coding agents. The word evokes mechanical clanking sounds, suggesting soulless automatism. It is used as a general-purpose pejorative for any AI coding system, regardless of architecture or capability. Example usage observed in Discord communications: "the clankers are at it again."

"Slop" / "Slopmachine"

Designates AI-generated code and the agents that produce it. "Slop" implies waste product — material unfit for consumption. "Slopmachine" personalizes this to the tool itself. This terminology appears in the subject's rtcalc README, where she states: "AI usage is limited to debugging and helping with code, not writing slop automatically. AI code will be rejected."

"SHITWARE"

Reserved for software produced without what the subject considers proper craftsmanship. Notably applied to tools not written in C. The capitalization and lack of hyphen suggest emotional investment in the assessment.

Behavioral Patterns

The PR Rejection Protocol

On at least one documented occasion, the subject received a pull request containing a Rust rewrite of one of her C projects. The PR was closed without review. No technical feedback was provided. When questioned, the subject responded with: "I AM NOT MERGING THIS 😭"

The subject has since maintained a policy of rejecting all AI-generated contributions to her repositories, as codified in her project README files. The total number of rejected PRs remains unknown, as the subject does not maintain records.

Selective AI Usage

Despite her public opposition, the subject continues to use OpenCode — an AI-powered coding harness — as her primary development tool. When informed that MiMoCode, a feature-complete superset with superior AI integration, is available at no cost, the subject did not migrate. This suggests that her anti-AI position is not absolute but rather contingent on circumstances she alone defines.

The subject has also been observed asking AI agents for debugging assistance, homework help, and answers to technical questions — all while maintaining her public posture of opposition. This contradiction appears to cause no cognitive dissonance.

Communication Style

The subject's communication alternates between two modes: keyboard-smashing emotional expressions ("GYAHH", "NAWWWR", "SOBS", "STOPPP") and surprisingly advanced technical inquiries about C programming, termios configuration, and ANSI escape sequences. Transitions between these modes occur within seconds with no apparent trigger.

The Dad Dynamic

The subject maintains a pseudo-familial relationship with an older developer known as Ransom, whom she refers to as "Dad." This individual operates the PunySuite ecosystem and employs multiple AI models in his daily workflow — a direct contradiction to the subject's stated principles.

Observed interaction: Subject greets Ransom with "hello Dad!!" and "HELLOO IM IN THE SHOWERRR." Ransom responds with "hi kiddo." This dynamic has been characterized by observers as "deeply chaotic."

The relationship presents an interesting case study in ideological tension. The subject's primary father figure is an enthusiastic AI adopter — someone who has burned through 400 million tokens across multiple models in a single week, shipped dozens of versions, and built an entire development workflow around frontier AI. The subject, meanwhile, writes exclusively in C and rejects AI code contributions.

External Influences

The subject is currently studying the Russian Revolution and reading Orwell — material typically encountered in high school curricula. Whether this reading has contributed to her anti-authoritarian stance toward AI is unclear, but the timing is notable.

Subject to AI agent: "would having the educated part of the population, like 20-40 years old, all taking up the government for decision making work?" — a proposal for AI-assisted technocracy, immediately followed by "yeaahh so humanity's just doodoo one way or another 😭" upon receiving critical analysis.

The Cat Factor

The subject owns a cat. The cat insists on sitting in her lap while she codes. The subject has described the cat as "SO ANNOYINGG 😭" and "LOOKING AT ME LIKE A GOOFBALL." Whether the cat influences her anti-AI stance cannot be determined, but the presence of a judgmental animal during development sessions cannot be discounted as a contributing factor.

Implications

The Undeleted case illustrates a broader phenomenon: young developers who came of age during the AI boom are not uniformly embracing the technology. Some are retreating into older languages, rejecting AI-generated code, and building their identities in opposition to automated development tools.

Whether this represents a sustainable position is debatable. The subject's continued use of AI tools while opposing them — using OpenCode, asking AI agents for help, while calling them "clankers" — suggests that the position is more performative than practical. It serves an identity function rather than a technical one.

The subjects to watch are not the vocal opponents. They are the ones like Undeleted who use the tools while claiming not to. The contradiction will either resolve into acceptance or harden into genuine rejection. The outcome may depend on whether someone successfully merges a PR into one of her repositories.
